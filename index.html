About Us

Ellen G. White (1827-1915) was an American Christian author and co-founder of the Seventh-day Adventist Church. She was born in Gorham, Maine, and raised in a Methodist family. At the age of 13, she experienced a traumatic injury that left her unconscious for several weeks. During this time, she had a series of visions that she believed were from God. Throughout her life, Ellen G. White claimed to have received over 2,000 visions and dreams. She saw her role as a messenger of God, and her writings are considered by Seventh-day Adventists to be inspired by God. In the 1840s, Ellen G. White became involved in the Millerite movement, a group of Christians who believed that the Second Coming of Jesus Christ was imminent. When the predicted date for the Second Coming passed without event, many Millerites were disappointed and disillusioned. However, Ellen G. White continued to have visions and began to develop a theology that would later become the basis for the Seventh-day Adventist Church. In 1863, the Seventh-day Adventist Church was officially founded, with Ellen G. White and her husband James White as two of its co-founders. Ellen G. White's writings, including her most famous book "The Great Controversy," continue to be widely read by Seventh-day Adventists today. Despite her influence on the Seventh-day Adventist Church, Ellen G. White's life and teachings have been the subject of controversy. Some critics have accused her of plagiarism and questioned the validity of her visions. However, many Seventh-day Adventists consider her to be a prophet and continue to hold her writings in high regard.
